Google Calendars: Material Design for Desktop

Material Design

PERSONAL MOCK-UP & GRAPHICS DESIGN EXERCISE

PROBLEM STATEMENT:
I love my android, and I love the Google Calendar to organize every detail of my life including timeboxing my work tasks and meal times. The only weakness to Android OS is that there are too many individual apps and services, and its language can be inconsistent accross different platforms. This is especially true when compared to iOS, where the cross-platform experience with iPhone and Mac is very well considered.

This cross-platform inconsistency is especially apparent with Google Calendars, and it's a pity since I am such a frequent user. Thus, I decided to re-imagine what Google Calendars will be like once it finally follows the Material Design language, with particular attention in solving the current inconsistencies in the visual display of information.


Above is my actual schedule saved on Google Calendars. I usually input a set schedule on the webapp with the luxury of the keyboard, and view my schedule throughout the week on my Android phone.

INCONSISTENCIES WITH CURRENT CALENDARS WEBAPP:

  • Inconsistent color coding with each event category
  • Inconsistent calendar views
  • Inconsistent layout

Inconsistent Color Coding: The phone app follows the color swatches as defined by the Material Design language. Each calendar activity category on the phone is thus color coded as such. However, since the webapp uses the old design language, the color coding is inconsistent. It is not apparent at first glance which events are within which calendar activity. Thus it is harder for the user to parse through the visual information on the webapp to, for example, find upcoming meetings and distinguish from university lectures.

Inconsistent Calendar Views: The phone app has Schedule, Day, 3 Day, Week, and Month calendar views. While the webapp has Day, Week, 4 Day, and Agenda. Views preferred by phone app users may be unavailable to the webapp, and vice versa. The inconsistent display of information forces the user an extra step in translating and interpreting the inconsistent visual information, while the content is really the same.

Inconsistent layout: The overall layout of the phone app is vastly different from the web app. Other than the fact that the webapp is less pleasant to look at, the inconsistency in visual display causes a dissonance in visual information, while both are really displaying the same content.

ADVANTAGES OF WEBAPP VIEW THAT SHOULD BE CONSIDERED
Other considerations in porting the calendar phone app to desktop is the extra screen space the web allows. This allows the following perks otherwise unavailable to the phone app:

  • Actually useful weekly view
  • Simultaneous display of schedule, events, and monthly calendar
  • Sidebar to display filters for easier parsing of information

FINAL MOCKUPS

CONCLUSIONS AND IMPROVEMENTS:
Above was a personal exercise to showcase that visual design language greatly affects the usability and user flow of a product, and not simply "it looks prettier."

However, while critiquing my own mock-ups, I realized that the margins for each calendar block is too wide. Particularily for the weekly view, where more information needs to be displayed, too much screen space is being wasted around the margins. Furthermore, while the seasons background is especially stylistic, it may be too overwhelming, and should be kept simpler so the style is more neutral to encompass a wider range of users' preferences.
